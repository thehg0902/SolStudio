# Persuasion Mechanics

## The Value Equation

```
        Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Success
Value = ────────────────────────────────────────────────
        Time to Result × Effort & Sacrifice
```

- Raise the numerator, shrink the denominator — every persuasive move maps to one of these four levers.
- Perception is **comparative**: always give a frame of reference (anchor), or the reader compares to zero.
- Quick emotional wins early increase perceived likelihood.

## The Two Human Motivators

1. **Away from pain** — usually the stronger driver. Make the pain of staying the same exceed the pain of change.
2. **Toward pleasure** — vivid, detailed, compelling dream state.

Amplify both with sensory information: sight, sound, touch, smell, taste. The more vivid the picture, the more powerful the pull.

## Attention

The brain filters everything through survival priorities. Attention goes to:

**Content of choice** (what the brain cares about):
- Avoiding threats and harm
- Obtaining food/resources
- Mating/attraction
- Maintaining tribal ties and gaining status

**Clues of choice** (how it spots them):
- Pattern interrupt — different from surroundings (color, boldness, motion)
- The unexplainable — things people can't immediately parse
- Conflict and drama
- Objective beauty — faces, nature, craftsmanship
- Matches previous experience — familiar positive patterns

When analyzing or creating a hook: start from a content of choice, express it through a clue of choice.

**Key mistakes to avoid**: pretending products are magic (products don't solve — solutions do; products enable solutions), and selling things instead of dream outcomes.

## Desire

**Desire cannot be created. It can only be found and amplified.**

Process: identify existing desire via market research → identify starting levels of desire/certainty/trust → craft copy that elevates each precisely to threshold.

Amplification moves:
- Stack the desire with self-esteem or self-actualization ("you deserve this")
- Contrast against the extreme opposite — what does the worst-case future look like if they don't act?
- Play with time — show both timelines
- Use imagery, color, sound
- Show that others desire it (social desire)
- Scarcity and urgency (only when genuinely true)

## Curiosity

Curiosity = the urge to close a knowledge gap. It's the dopamine engine of long copy.

**The value loop**: promise/tease interesting information → reader takes the desired micro-action → deliver the promised payoff → open the next loop (cliffhanger) → repeat. The copy becomes the reader's dopamine source.

**Amplifiers (6):**
1. Extreme specificity ("6 design fixes before month-end worth ~$2,000")
2. NOT statements — tell them what they believe is actually wrong
3. Associate with things they're already curious about
4. Paradoxes — questions they can't answer ("training 3 hours a day and still no results?")
5. Conflict and drama
6. FOMO — in-group vs out-group

**Fascination formulas** (headline/bullet starters):
How to… · The secret to… · Why… · What… · What never… · Plus… · [Number]… · …Right? WRONG. · Warning:… · Are you…? · The [gimmick] that… · The sneaky… · [Direct benefit in timeframe] · Did you know [specific stat]? · If [pain], then [payoff] · When [surprising condition]… · The quickest/easiest/safest way to… · The truth about… · Better than [known solution]… · The single step…

## Trust & Authority

**Primal/tribal indicators**: composed confidence, abundance (not needy), visible competence, existing following (social proof), "I already have what you want."

**Other indicators**: association with respected authorities, credentials from trusted sources, longevity in the space, demonstrated superiority in a key metric, having achieved the result yourself.

**Trust-building transactions**: make a promise → act on it → honor it → trust levels up. Repeat. Under-promise, over-deliver. Set expectations for testing periods and revision windows.

**The against-self-interest move**: recommend the option that's worse for you and better for them — "I'd make more selling you the big package, but this smaller one fits your situation better." Massive trust spike.

**Reveal a better way of seeing the world**: teaching someone a genuinely new, useful frame creates deep trust.

**Trust red flags to eliminate from all copy and conversation:**
- Salesman markers (triggers their past bad sales experiences)
- Pushing the sale too early — let them buy, don't sell
- Recommending before understanding their situation
- Desperation / over-eagerness
- Evading questions and concerns

## Belief (Certainty)

Operational sequence:
1. List every belief the reader must hold to take the action
2. List every claim the copy makes
3. Attach proof to each claim

**Proof toolkit:**
- Claim → proof pattern, repeated (big claim → evidence → next claim → evidence)
- Showing > telling — demonstrate the result
- Layman's explanation — so simple a 10-year-old can't argue ("If you need customers, and I bring customers, this works")
- Metaphor — connect the mechanism to something they already understand
- Social proof — others believe it, so it's probably true
- Outside respected sources — studies, credible press, neutral parties *respected by this specific audience*

**"Will it work for ME?"** — everyone believes their situation is special. Answer with niching: tailor the promise to their exact situation and speak their tribe's language ("the trucker's weight-loss plan built around gas-station food").

**Belief language** (implies causality, quietly raises certainty): If… then… · The reason why… · Because… · This leads to… · When…

**Tone of certainty**: humans are tuning forks — calm, absolute certainty in voice and text triggers a sympathetic state of belief. It transfers through writing.

## Empathy Protocol

"If you show someone you understand their situation and tailored a solution to it, they will trust you far more."

- Self-awareness → self-analysis: examine how your own thoughts form; others' minds work similarly
- Immerse in their worldview without judging it — every worldview has a reason
- The single most important WWP question is **"Where are they at right now?"** — live a day in their life mentally before writing a word

## Tribal Marketing

Humans buy to join, signal, and rise within tribes.

**Elements of a tribe**: leader · members (each with a role) · values/rules/culture · activities · purpose · enemies (us vs. them) · resources/territory.

**How people choose tribes**: Does the purpose resonate? Do the benefits match my desires? Do the values align with mine? Do I want to be like the leader/members? Is this tribe winning against other tribes?

**Use in copy:**
- Signal tribal membership ("for GTA homeowners who…")
- Offer status boosts within their existing tribe
- Social proof from inside the tribe (reviews from people just like them)
- Never ask them to buy something that jeopardizes their tribal status
