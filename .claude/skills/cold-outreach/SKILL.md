# Cold Outreach

## Frame First (WOSS Principles)

Outreach fails on frame before it fails on words.

- **Never approach from weakness.** The party with high demand and scarce supply controls the relationship. Engineer scarcity: be a person of value with limited slots.
- **Be different.** Sound like a professional peer, not a vendor. If you write like a professional, speak like one, and hold standards like one — you are one.
- **Avoid desperation.** They can smell it. Talk less. Evaluate *them* first. Speak from the position of the one doing the selecting.
- **Be willing to walk away.** The person willing to walk away wins every negotiation.
- **Respect your own time** as if it costs thousands per hour — it changes how you write and who you pursue.
- **Demand generates demand.** State or imply that others want your work. Perceived demand raises perceived value.
- **Relevance is everything.** People need a reason. Make every message unmistakably about THEM — their business, their situation, their opportunity. A compliment only works when it's specific enough that it could only apply to them.

## The Reason Why

Every message answers, in the first two lines: *why this business, why now, why you.* Generic openers mark the message as spam before sentence two.

## The 5 Fatal Outreach Mistakes

1. **Being salesy** — be a human; lead with their problem, offer a solution, back with proof
2. **Skipping proof** — use your case study; without one, use the local advantage ("I live here; my family needed exactly this service and couldn't find you")
3. **Lazy templates** — the path of least resistance is not a winning strategy; personalize the diagnosis
4. **Focusing on yourself** — they don't care about you, only what you can do for their life
5. **Grammar mistakes** — instant credibility death

## Message Structure (email/DM)

1. **Personalized reason for contact** — a specific, true observation about their business
2. **Credibility** — case study result, or a sharp insight that proves you understand their market
3. **Curiosity tease** — name the value, withhold the mechanism ("I'd be happy to walk you through exactly how this campaign worked")
4. **Single low-friction CTA** — "How does your calendar look later this week?" / "Reply and I'll send the breakdown"

### Case-study template skeleton
> Hi [Name],
> [Specific reason for reaching out to *them*]
> I just finished [result: metric + timeframe] for [same-niche business].
> Happy to show you behind the scenes of the campaign.
> How does your calendar look later this week?

Keep it short. Curiosity does the heavy lifting — outreach *is* a fascination; the full answer lives on the other side of the CTA.

## Cold Call Structure

1. **Opener + permission**: "Hi [Owner], this is [Name] — I work with local [niche] businesses on getting more clients. I know you're busy so I'll keep it brief — do you have a few minutes, or is another time better?"
2. **Positioning line**: one sentence on who you help and how
3. **Problem questions** (before ANY pitch — let them talk themselves into needing help):
   - "Is your practice currently able to handle an extra X bookings a month?"
   - "What does your current process for attracting your highest-paying clients look like?"
   - "A lot of [niche] owners tell me they struggle with [researched pain] — is that true for you too?"
4. **CTA**: "I'd love to grab 15 minutes later this week to show you what's working for practices like yours — fair enough?"
5. **Looping**: each objection → acknowledge → answer → return to the CTA

### Gatekeeper handling
Receptionists screen. "It's personal" backfires. What works: "It's an opportunity — I'm not with a company and I'm not selling anything; I just need two minutes with [owner]." Persist politely; get the owner's direct line or best callback time.

### Hard-learned rule
Jumping to "let's schedule a call" too early reads pushy. Problem questions first — the meeting request should feel like the natural next step of a conversation *they're* invested in.

## Reply Handling

- **"Just send some info"** → "Sure thing 👇" + 2-minute Loom walking broad strokes of the campaign, teasing the specifics for a call
- **"What's the price?"** → "I generally do performance-based pricing so incentives are aligned — but specifics depend on whether you have the requirements in place. Open to a quick call, or is this not something you want to pursue?"
- **"Not interested"** → "No worries — have a great day!" Clean exit, zero neediness. Log and move on.

## Follow-Up Cadence

- **+48 hours**: simple check-in (one line)
- **+1 week**: walk-away message ("I'll assume the timing isn't right — door's open if that changes"). The takeaway often converts.
- **Booked calls**: send a calendar invite immediately + a reminder the morning of

## Volume & Iteration

- Daily outreach blocks; consistent volume beats sporadic bursts
- Track: sent → opened/answered → replied → booked → closed
- OODA loop weekly: which niche, which angle, which subject lines move the numbers — kill losers, scale winners
- After prospecting, periodically pick ONE high-fit company and invest deep research + free value — the deep-personalization play converts at a different tier than volume plays
