# Copy Frameworks

## Pre-flight

- WWP complete (see winners-writing-process skill). No WWP → no draft.
- **One objective per asset**: ad → click; email → reply; landing page → lead; sales call → sale. Never stack objectives.
- Give the reader a legitimate, objective reason to stop and look, and a solid reason to act now with no excuse.

## Short-Form Frameworks

### DIC — Disrupt · Intrigue · Click
Best for: cold/unaware audiences (Level 1–2 awareness), paid social ads.
- **Disrupt**: pattern interrupt tied to their pain/desire
- **Intrigue**: open a knowledge gap they need closed
- **Click**: single CTA that promises the payoff

### PAS — Problem · Amplify · Solution
Best for: problem-aware audiences (Level 2).
- **Problem**: call it out in their exact language
- **Amplify**: consequences, timelines, emotional cost of inaction
- **Solution**: present the mechanism, then the product as its vehicle

### HSO — Hook · Story · Offer
Versatile; best for warm audiences, emails, organic content.
- **Hook**: curiosity + relevance + drama + hint of value
- **Story**: relatable transformation (mini hero's journey)
- **Offer**: natural conclusion of the story

## Long-Form Structure (landing pages, sales pages, VSLs)

### Lead
- Hook attention on the dominant pain/desire from research
- Position the copy itself as the vehicle to their goal
- Establish baseline trust (who is this, why should I listen)

### Body
- Walk the chain: pain → roadblock → solution → mechanism
- Boost certainty and trust with claim → proof pattern
- Amplify pain/desire at intervals to maintain momentum

### Close
- Introduce the product as the best implementation of the solution
- CTA (see objections-and-closes skill)
- Demolish remaining objections after the first CTA

## Hooks

Every hook needs all four:
1. Curiosity trigger
2. Relevance to *this* reader ("how is this related to me?")
3. Drama or conflict
4. Hint of value on the other side

## Storytelling

The hero's journey compressed for copy:
call to adventure → leave the known world → transformation begins → the abyss (everything fails) → rebirth → return victorious.

Requirements:
- **Relatable** — the character starts where the reader is now
- **Conflict and tension** — obstacles that mirror the reader's roadblocks
- **Connected to their desires** — hero gets what the reader wants
- **Regular twists** — zig, zig, ZAG
- **Immersive** — sensory detail paints the movie in their head

## Formatting Rules

- **Hook on hook**: every line's only job is to get the next line read
- One idea per block; short paragraphs; whitespace is a weapon
- A well-placed image beats a wall of words
- Big blobs of text kill attention — break by idea, not by length
- Answer the reader's internal questions in order: *What is this? → Is it for me? → Will it work for me? → How much effort/risk?*

## Identity Products

Some products don't solve an active problem — they let the reader satisfy a basic desire in a new way and adopt a new identity/status. For these: sell the identity and the status change, not a problem-solution chain.

## Tone Matching

Match tone to the avatar's self-perspective. For frustrated, hardworking audiences: "you deserve better — your hard work should be paying off." Give them a taste of the desire early; never lecture them with the hard truth head-on.

## Refinement Checklist

First drafts are always rough. Refinement is where copy is made.

1. Kill the three sins: **confusing · boring · irrelevant**
2. Relevance must be *now* — aimed at them today, not last month, not someday
3. Physical reset (walk away, train, then re-read cold)
4. Re-read *as the avatar*: what movie plays in their head line by line?
5. Get outside readers who match the avatar to react
6. For each line ask: what effect does this create in the reader's mind, and is it the effect I want?
7. OODA the result: Observe → Orient → Decide → Act, on repeat until it converts
